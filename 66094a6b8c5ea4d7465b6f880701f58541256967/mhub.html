MHub


2019 Google Design Exercise

Overview

At the University of Michigan, new students often struggle to understand the nature of the student organizations in a process of searching and browsing both online and offline. After conducting user interviews, as well as usability tests, I have identified three major problems in centralizing, discovering and understanding online information. With more than 1,400+ student organizations at Michigan, online information is outdated and scattered across many different platforms. Therefore, I designed a hub where new students can explore and discover their future student clubs, with aggregated images and posts from social media, and can pitch their new student group ideas.

Platform: Responsive Web Application

Year: 2019

Duration: 1 Week

Tools: Sketch, InVison, Illustrator

Overall Process

Problem Scope – User Interviews – Analysis – Brainstorming – Low-Fi Prototyping – Usability Tests – Refinement - Mid/High-Fi Prototyping - Deliverables

Design Brief

A new school year is approaching and the University of Michigan orientation team from the Office of New Student Programs is looking to you for some design expertise. Design an experience for new students to browse, search, and propose new student organizations.

Problem Scope

Identifying the possible users

Before taking any design process steps, I thought it was crucial to align myself with the context and the objective of the design exercise. First, I assumed I was designing for the Orientation Team from Office of New Student Programs, so I could use all accessible resources around me.

On the next step, I identified who I was designing for. Even though 70% of 9,500 total new students are undergraduates at Michigan, I didn’t limit myself to just designing for the new undergrads. Therefore, I decided to go deep into all incoming undergrad, master’s, and PhD students at the University of Michigan.

User Research

After identifying my users, I decided to conduct user interviews instead of surveys. Even though I knew I could gain a lot of data in a short time frame with surveys, I needed a deep understanding of users’ experiences in searching and proposing new student clubs. Therefore, I set up 6 user interviews: 2 from undergrad, master’s, and PhD programs.

During my interviews, I asked questions primarily focused on understanding a journey from being admitted to Michigan, the first day of orientation, to discovering and proposing new student organizations of their interests. I thought it was important to get a full scope of experiences behind searching for new student groups. Therefore, I decided to begin my questions from being admitted to Michigan. Also, I went deeper with any frustrations and challenges they have encountered during their journey with follow-up questions.
